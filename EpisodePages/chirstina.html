Episode 4

Why representation
and diversity in
leadership matters

Christina Ryan

In our fourth episode of Global Dialogue Series on Neurodiversity, we speak with Christina Ryan, the CEO and Founder of the Disability Leadership Institute. Christina has been an active leader in the Australian disability community for over 20 years, working at an international, national and local level to change the diversity agenda, while mentoring and supporting numerous people with disabilities to achieve their own leadership success.

Christina established the Disability Leadership Institute in 2016 as a professional hub to build & support leaders with disabilities.Christina pioneered the use of mainstream forums by women with disabilities at the United Nations, and now mentors and teaches effective use of the UN for rights activists globally, while working as a leadership coach for people with disabilities.

Many organizations have not given a thought about how disabilities and leadership can actually go together. Christina tells us it’s either because they think people with disabilities can't do leadership or they are not noticing them.

In her years of experience, she observed that not many people talk about their disabilities in the workplace. The main reason for this is that it can stop one’s career or promotion. She has seen that opening up about their disability has been a risky thing to do.

The more senior level they are in, the greater the risk. They are the ones at the most risk of losing their job if they speak about their differences, be encouraged to look for new jobs or pushed to the sidelines and never given any opportunities.

There is a certain level up to which people with disabilities get a job but beyond that, there’s no further growth. We seem to be trapped into thinking that disability is something that happens in the lower job positions and not in leadership positions.Many internal leadership programs focus on women, people from different cultures but never talk or recognize disabilities as part of their leadership program and that should be the first step to shift their mindsets to start thinking about disabilities in the context of leadership as part of their diversity.

Christina tells us that the best way to solve this is for people to understand their differences, use it as an asset in their workplace, which will make it easy for them to be open about how they think or operate.

To develop neurodiverse leadership, organizations need to give flexibility for individuals to talk about what they are good at and equally about what they are not good at.

They should be able to delegate the work they struggle with to someone who is good at that task without any repercussions and truly embrace people’s differences.

Organizations should think about what diversity really means to them, understand how diversity is contributing, how everyone is not the same and celebrate those differences. We need diverse approaches and diverse thoughts, not just in the workforce but in leadership too.

We need all those different people shaping the way the modern workforce will look into the future, so we can grow diversity and have different decision makers at the top of the organization.Christina shares how people with disabilities spend a lot of time just on the edges. It’s quite understandable that they will always be conscious about how they are not included or a part of the group.

It's a difficult task for them to change the culture inside the room when they are the ones outside of it. People should not feel responsible for making their differences be ok, or try to make other people comfortable by hiding their disabilities so it’s less confronting.

It should be the people who matter in the organization, people with the power, people with the resources, coming to them and asking what they need, what it should look like, rather than expecting people with disabilities to change the system from outside.

When an organization supports their staff with disabilities to do leadership development, it changes a few things. It changes how the organization looks at that person, how the person operates and makes them more comfortable with themselves.

Role models are very important. When you have a person with a disability in a senior position, all those entry-level employees can see the potential of their growth in the company and also see that it’s acceptable to be different.

It’s very isolating when you’re the only person in the entire branch or department who has a disability. This is still a very common experience but it’s gradually shifting. We start to see a shift when people with disabilities become recognized and celebrated within the organizations

When people are being themselves, they work better, they are not trying to work on something that drains their energy or pretending to be someone else. Leaders and senior executives should look around themselves and ask if their leadership team is diverse and represents all individuals across gender, race, disability & cognitive abilities, if they can’t see anyone they should ask why not and what they are doing to make it change.
