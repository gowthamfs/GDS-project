Episode 7

From the Land of Autism

Yenn Purkis

Imagine someone from a faraway land who speaks a different language and has a different culture than yours comes to your country and tries to learn your language and your culture. Would you appreciate that person for trying to adapt or demean them that they are not capable? Our guest today, Yenn Purkis tells us how they view autism as a cultural difference and people from the land of Autism communicate and work differently and that there are more ways than one to communicate.

The biggest challenge according to them is to educate neurotypical people that their language, even though different, is equally valid. When we start looking at autism as a cultural difference, we start looking at it in a more positive light. We understand that there are differences in approach and accept that there are different ways of thinking, and thus, truly embrace neurodiversity.

Yenn Purkis is an autistic and non-binary author, public speaker and community leader from Australia who also has a diagnosis of schizophrenia. Yenn works in the autism / neurodiversity, gender diversity and mental health advocacy spaces. Yenn is the author of ten published books on elements of autism and is a regular blogger. They were named the 2016 ACT Volunteer of the Year and received the ACT Chief Minister’s Inclusion Award for Achievement in Inclusion in 2019.

Yenn is also a prolific public speaker and has presented at a range of events including TEDx Canberra in 2013. One of the things that I’m really amazed about Yenn, is their resilience and motivation to make a positive change in life, no matter how difficult the journey is. Yenn had an extremely hard life when they were a young adult, having schizophrenia, generalized anxiety disorder and depression making life very arduous, but that didn’t stop them.

At the age of 25, Yenn realized that they had to change their life and set out on this amazing journey from being unemployable to having a career as a public servant in government administration and publishing ten books. As Yenn shares, they are a very ethical person with a very unethical history and that has pushed and motivated them to make a difference not just in their life but also in the lives of others.

In 2005, Yenn wrote their first autobiography titled “Finding a Different Kind of Normal” thanks to the motivation from Polly Samuel, who is also known as Donna Williams. Polly is one of the first autistic people in the world to write an autobiography. While Yenn was reluctant to write about their life story initially, it was Polly who helped them to change their mind. Polly told Yenn how when she gives talks to parents, there are always a few parents at the back of the room, who sit there and leave before the talk ends.

Those are the parents of autistic kids who are in trouble with the law and are ashamed to talk about their children. If Yenn wrote their autobiography, it would be for those parents. This made Yenn think about their own parents and their experiences and set out to write their first book. Yenn did not advocate much until 2012, when they met a young autistic man who refused to believe Yenn’s life story was true. He could not believe it because till then, every conversation he had around autism was all about what he couldn’t do or his lack of capabilities, that he needed a lot of support and couldn’t live independently.

At that moment, Yenn realized that there were many more autistic individuals like this young man, who were always reminded about what they cannot do. According to Yenn, the diagnosis of autism should lead to positive things like getting the right support, being empowered and becoming proud of who you are. This catapulted Yenn to the world of advocacy and inspired them to write their second book to help autistic teenagers prepare for the workforce. Since Yenn’s diagnosis of autism in 1994, there has been a paradigm shift in the community, from how parents embrace autism, how the general population has become more aware and educated about autism, to young neurodivergent kids, teenagers and adults achieving amazing things and advocating for themselves.

Yenn advises young neurodivergent adults that while the journey might be hard, it does get better, not incrementally better, but with some ups and downs. As you get older and the more aware you become of who you are, how you communicate, how you fit in the world and who you want to be, life does get better. In Yenn’s life, the pivotal moment was when they realized that they were in charge of what happened in their life, that they were in the driver’s seat and it was up to them to make the change. Within eight years, Yenn achieved all the goals they set out to do.
