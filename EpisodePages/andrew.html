Episode 3

Building resources
and ecosystem for
neurodiversity success

Andrew Eddy

In our third episode of Global Dialogue Series on Neurodiversity, we speak with Andrew Eddy. Andrew Eddy is the CEO of Untapped, a social enterprise focused on developing a sustainable neurodiverse employment ecosystem to increase opportunities for autistic individuals.

Andrew is on a mission to change the narrative about autism through awareness, education and engagement. Andrew leads the Neurodiversity Hub initiative, which collaborates with universities, colleges, employers and service providers to work together to create environments and opportunities for neurodivergent young adults to succeed.

There’s a growing interest towards neurodiversity and the recognition that diverse talents make companies more innovative. One of the most untapped talents is neurodiversity and at least one in ten people are neurodivergent.

More than ever, it’s crucial for companies to assess their workplace for sensory issues, make sure their jobs are appropriately designed and to provide resource groups and awareness training about neurodiversity.

Even within the organization, there are many neurodiverse individuals who might not identify themselves or share their concerns Andrew tells us that the best way to solve this is by implementing programs that can support neurodiverse individuals.

More than policies, support programs help to bring out better integration as it’s not just words anymore but action. Andrew has seen huge impacts with the companies that he has worked with when such programs were implemented and how it brought more people together to support and embrace neurodiversity. Hiring neurodiverse individuals can be a challenge with the existing hiring models.

One of the things that I completely agree with Andrew is using learning as continuous assessment to hire neurodiverse talents. That’s something that we also do in HashHackCode for neurodivergent tech talents. Andrew shares with us how when hiring neurodiverse individuals, it’s important not to look at job descriptions but rather their traits and how this can be achieved through assessment workshops.

The bootcamps that can help identify the potential of the neurodiverse candidate. He also tells us that neurodiversity should not be seen purely as an HR initiative, but rather as a part of the company's capabilities.

This will help to integrate neurodivergent talents into the workforce much more easily as the requirement is coming from the people who are going to work with them and they will be able to better see their growth and nurture their talent.

This aspect is really important because when it comes to the growth of neurodiverse individuals in a company, there hasn’t been much done and that is something we have to really change. Most neurodiverse individuals are only given a job, there’s no recognition after that.

Within the company, there should be programs that help neurodiverse individuals not only get a job but identify and understand their strengths, build on those strengths, look for other opportunities in the company and advocate for themselves.

One of the best places to start for neurodiversity is to build employee resource groups, which can help transform the narrative by changing the conversation and creating more awareness A good tip from Andrew was to take part in various summits and webinars on neurodiversity and encourage people to watch and use it as a base to develop discussions.

Andrews adds how during this pandemic period, we have all had difficult times and mental health has become very important, especially for neurodiverse individuals whose challenges are even more magnified, the support and understanding of managers and co-workers is essential for neurodiverse individuals. It can vary from technical to life skills to mental health. Andrew and his team have created some great resources that can help companies to integrate neurodiversity, make universities more accessible to neurodiverse individuals and help neurodiverse individuals succeed.
