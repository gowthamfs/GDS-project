Episode 5

Becoming a
Neurodiverse Superhero

Leena Haque

A superhero is someone who inspires us, who shows hope when there’s none, who makes sure others don’t suffer the same struggle that they went through and breaks barriers with all their might. Someone who stands up for the injustice against the community, someone who no matter how many times they fall, will rise up to fight and give their best. That’s exactly what our guest today, Leena Haque has been doing.Leena is a Senior UX Designer & Neurodiversity Program Lead at BBC. As a neurodivergent individual, she overcame many barriers from education to employment and is working towards breaking the same barriers for others using innovation and creativity.

She has made significant contributions to the betterment of the neurodiverse community locally, nationally and globally.At a time when Neurodiversity was virtually unknown, Leena’s dedication and persistence delivered an engaging narrative for change and support. Leena co-led BBC's Neurodiversity initiative CAPE (Creating a Positive Environment) which she has nurtured and grown from a small, temporary project within the Finance department to an organization-wide program.Leena is recognized as a key influencer in the media and creative sectors, she has received multiple awards for her work in BBC CAPE from the National Autistic Society, the Business Disability Forum and Creative Diversity Network.

As a child, Leena struggled to fit in with her peers. When she was old enough to grasp what it meant to be different from her peers, she understood that school was not going to be easy for her. She felt her future was uncertain because of her learning differences, struggling to keep up with the education system and the curriculum. But with the support of her family and good teachers, she eventually succeeded in school and overcame the hurdles in her education and found success in her career.As a neurodiverse individual, Leena has always felt she had to work harder than the rest of her colleagues and it was difficult for her to be taken seriously because of her neurological differences and neurodiversity.

The struggle for neurodiverse individuals is not just about finding a job that allows them to use their talents but it’s also about being taken seriously and truly recognized for their skillset.Though it was a journey of many ups and downs, she understood herself better, how to manage her time better, what her strengths are and the areas she found challenging. During this journey, Leena made a promise to herself that others would not face the same struggles and barriers that she faced in education or career.

how no child or young person regardless of their abilities should go through the feeling that their future is uncertain. She is striving to make sure every child feels happy and has a safe future.Success is not a linear path, you will have many successes and failures as you progress in your life. Leena shares how while her career growth has been good due to the understanding and support from her co-workers and senior leaders, she still has a long way to go.

People tend to think that because Leena is at BBC, she was successful but that’s not the truth. Leena shares how there are still struggles and barriers she faces and she continues to put in hard work and have conversations to make sure the environment is accessible and all are treated fairly.Leena is open about her failure because not many people are, as there’s a stigma to talking about rejection and failure. Just recently she was not successful in an assessment for leadership growth.

She hopes people will understand that rejection is a part of life and others will be able to relate to her, and realize that they are not alone or that it might not even be their fault as sometimes the system is designed to fail people who are different.This recent rejection was hard for Leena. She felt like she had let her family and colleagues down. Momentarily she even felt like giving up and thought she might never make it but that did not stop her. She picked herself up, told that there is new hope, new light in the darkness and kept going.

On asking what motivates Leena to keep fighting against these barriers, she shared how kindness and helping others motivates her.Most organizations don't realize that they are creating barriers for people. It takes people like Leena to bring it to their attention that there is a barrier and it’s preventing someone from growing. For neurodiversity to thrive, organizations need to create a safe place for neurodiverse individuals where they can share their concerns, thoughts and be able to flourish in the workplace.

Most systems and processes are designed for the neurotypical mind and they are filtering out neurodivergent thinkers and talent. To prevent losing people right at that start, a real review has to take place.In Leena’s case, the recent assessment consisted of vague tasks and methods using psychometric tests, role-plays, and situational judgment tests. Anyone who is neurodivergent will understand that these methods can be challenging and are not designed for the neurodivergent mind but rather for the neurotypical mind.

Moreover, these types of assessments are subjective and may not give an accurate picture of what people will do in real life.Every superhero needs a team to support them. With the help of her brilliant line manager Sean, who is also the co-lead of the BBC CAPE initiative with her, Leena has been able to find ways to contribute to the organization, grow as a person and professionally. Leena sees failure as a foundation to build from. We might end up feeling empty at that moment but Leena tells us how just like every house is built from an empty piece of land, we too can build from this experience.
